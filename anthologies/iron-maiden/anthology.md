# Iron Maiden: The Complete Anthology

## *"Up the Irons"* — Seven Volumes Spanning Forty-Six Years of Unreasonable Ambition

---

### Liner Notes

*by Nick Kent, for the NME*

---

One spent the better part of two decades nursing the unshakeable conviction that Iron Maiden were beneath serious consideration. One was wrong. There. I've said it. That was not, I assure you, a painless admission, and the fact that I'm making it at all should tell you something about the scale of the error.

The NME did not cover Iron Maiden. We did not cover the New Wave of British Heavy Metal. We did not cover any of it, because we had decided — with the serene, unexamined certainty that only the truly parochial can muster — that punk had rendered all of this redundant. While Geoff Barton at Sounds was filing breathless dispatches from the Ruskin Arms in East Ham about a bass player called Steve Harris who had something, we were in Camden writing about Orange Juice. I don't say this to disparage Orange Juice. I say it because Orange Juice, for all their undeniable charm, are not performing to a quarter of a million people in Rio de Janeiro thirty years later. Harris is.

Here is what we got wrong: we assumed that because the music was loud and the audience was working-class and the imagery involved a cartoon skeleton, it could not possibly be intelligent. We were snobs. We were catastrophically, unforgivably wrong. Harris, that galumphing East London autodidact with his galloping bass and his improbable reading list, was writing songs about Samuel Taylor Coleridge and George Orwell and Frank Herbert and the Charge of the Light Brigade, and he was performing them to eighty thousand people who would not otherwise have encountered any of these things, and he was doing it with a sincerity and a commitment to craft that should have shamed every dilettante on the King's Road.

While we were busy genuflecting at the altar of post-punk austerity, Maiden were performing Coleridge to eighty thousand people. While we were championing bands who would fold after two singles and a John Peel session, Harris was building something that would outlast every last one of them. The terrible irony, of course, is that they were doing precisely what we always claimed to want from rock music — they were taking it seriously.

Bruce Dickinson arrived in 1982 and the band became extraordinary. An operatic fencer who would later fly the band's own 747. You cannot make this up. You cannot parody it either, because parody requires a gap between pretension and achievement, and with Maiden there is no gap. They are exactly what they claim to be, and they have been exactly that for forty-six years, and if that doesn't constitute a kind of genius then I no longer know what the word means.

What follows is seven volumes spanning the entirety of that career. It includes the Paul Di'Anno years, which are rawer and punker than anything we championed at the time. It includes the Blaze Bayley years, which are darker and more interesting than anyone gave them credit for. It includes the reunion albums, which are progressive rock by people who will never admit to being progressive rock. And it includes the live recordings, because you cannot understand this band from studio recordings alone — you need to hear what happens when they play "Fear of the Dark" to a hundred thousand people and a hundred thousand people sing it back.

I was wrong about Iron Maiden. The NME was wrong about Iron Maiden. The entire British music press was wrong about Iron Maiden. The evidence is definitive and the verdict is not appealable.

Up the Irons, as they say. I never thought I'd write that. But here we are.

— *N.K., London, 2026*

## Volume I: Iron Dawn (1978–1981)

*The Di'Anno years. Punk-metal from the streets of East London.*

> Paul Di'Anno was a skinhead from Leytonstone with a voice like a football hooligan discovering he could actually sing, and for two albums he was exactly what this band needed. The debut was recorded at Kingsway Studios for the kind of money that wouldn't cover a London cab fare, and "Phantom of the Opera" — seven minutes of galloping bass, twin-guitar harmonics, and tempo changes that would make a prog band blush — announced that Harris had ambitions far beyond the three-chord thrash that the NWOBHM label implied.
>
> "Prowler" opens the whole thing with a riff that sounds like someone breaking into a building. "Running Free" was the first single and it's punk with bass runs. "Transylvania" is an instrumental that nobody asked for and everybody needed. *Killers* was tighter, darker, Martin Birch behind the desk giving Harris the production he deserved — "Wrathchild" and "Murders in the Rue Morgue" are the sound of a band that knows it's about to become enormous.
>
> Di'Anno was sacked in 1981 for, depending on who you ask, cocaine, unreliability, or not being Bruce Dickinson. The B-sides and bonus tracks here — "Burning Ambition," "Twilight Zone," "Invasion" — prove there was more to the early years than the albums alone suggest.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume II: Ascension (1982–1985)

*The Number of the Beast through Powerslave. The golden age.*

> Dickinson walked in and everything changed. The voice — that absurd, operatic, air-raid-siren voice — turned Harris's compositions from very good heavy metal into something approaching the genuinely epic. *The Number of the Beast* was the announcement: "Hallowed Be Thy Name" is seven minutes of existential dread set to a galloping riff and it is, I will concede without further argument, one of the great rock songs of the 1980s. The title track got them accused of Satanism by people who had not listened to the lyrics, which is a tradition that continues to this day.
>
> *Piece of Mind* brought Nicko McBrain and "The Trooper," which is the Battle of Balaclava as recounted by someone who has actually read Tennyson. *Powerslave* is the peak of the classic era — "Aces High" opens with Churchill's "We Shall Fight Them" speech and then proceeds to demonstrate exactly what fighting sounds like. And "Rime of the Ancient Mariner" — thirteen minutes and forty-five seconds of Coleridge, in which Harris proves he is not merely referencing literature but genuinely adapting it, with a quiet middle section that quotes the poem directly and then builds back into the most devastating crescendo in the band's catalogue.
>
> "Total Eclipse," the B-side that should have been on *The Number of the Beast* instead of "Gangland," is included here because the fans were right and the band were wrong. The covers — "Cross-Eyed Mary," "Rainbow's Gold" — show what they sound like when they're having fun.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume III: Somewhere in Time (1986–1989)

*Synth guitars, the concept album, and the peak of ambition.*

> This is the point at which Harris decided that heavy metal was not enough. *Somewhere in Time* added guitar synthesisers, which got him accused of selling out by people who hadn't noticed that the songs were still seven minutes long and still about science fiction novels. "Wasted Years" is the single, and it's beautiful — Adrian Smith's finest moment, a meditation on touring and the passage of time that manages to be anthemic without being stupid. "Alexander the Great" is eight and a half minutes about the Macedonian conqueror and it has never been played live, which has given it a cult status that borders on the religious.
>
> And then *Seventh Son of a Seventh Son* — the concept album, the thing that was not supposed to work and absolutely did. The Evil That Men Do. Can I Play with Madness. The Clairvoyant. Infinite Dreams. The title track at nearly ten minutes. This is Iron Maiden at their most musically sophisticated, and I use that word deliberately, because sophistication is not something the NME ever associated with this band and we were, once again, wrong.
>
> The B-sides from this era — "Reach Out," "That Girl," "Juanita," "Black Bart Blues," "Massacre" — are looser, funnier, proof that a band this serious about their art can also have a laugh. "The Sheriff of Huddersfield" is, I believe, about cricket.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume IV: The Darkening (1990–1999)

*Bruce departs, Blaze arrives. The difficult years and the hidden masterworks.*

> Here is where it gets complicated, and here is where the anthology earns its keep, because the conventional narrative — Bruce leaves, everything falls apart, Blaze Bayley can't sing, nothing to see — is lazy, reductive, and wrong.
>
> *No Prayer for the Dying* and *Fear of the Dark* are the transitional albums, deliberately stripped back after the *Seventh Son* grandiosity, and they contain material that deserves better than its reputation. "Afraid to Shoot Strangers" is a Gulf War meditation of genuine emotional depth. "Fear of the Dark" — the title track — has become one of the great live anthems, a hundred thousand people singing that chorus in the dark, which is an achievement that transcends whatever you think of the studio version. "Judas Be My Guide" is a hidden gem that Dave Murray aficionados cite as proof of his underrated songwriting.
>
> And then Blaze. Poor, maligned, unfairly dismissed Blaze Bayley, whose voice was not Dickinson's voice and whose crime was to stand in front of a band whose audience would never fully accept him. But listen — actually *listen* — to "Sign of the Cross." Eleven minutes and eighteen seconds of the darkest, most doom-laden music Harris ever wrote, and Bayley delivers it with a conviction that borders on the harrowing. "The Clansman" — which Dickinson later reclaimed and made a live staple — is epic, stirring, Braveheart-as-heavy-metal, and it was Bayley who recorded it first.
>
> *The X Factor* is a difficult, rewarding album that sounds like a band going through hell and refusing to compromise. "Fortunes of War," "The Edge of Darkness," "The Unbeliever" — this is Harris at his most introspective. "Como Estais Amigos," the closer of *Virtual XI*, is a song about the Falklands War written in Spanish, which is about as far from commercial calculation as it is possible to get.
>
> The covers here — "Doctor Doctor," "My Generation" — are, respectively, the best UFO cover ever recorded and a version of the Who that Pete Townshend would have approved of.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume V: Renaissance (2000–2006)

*The reunion. Three guitars. The progressive rebirth.*

> Dickinson came back. Adrian Smith came back. Janick Gers stayed. Three guitars. And "The Wicker Man" opened *Brave New World* like a dam breaking — the sound of a band that had been apart for six years and was furious about the time wasted. The three-guitar lineup was supposed to be unwieldy and instead it was magnificent, layering harmonies and countermelodies that gave Harris the orchestral palette he'd always wanted.
>
> *Brave New World* is the reunion album and it is faultless. "Ghost of the Navigator" builds for seven minutes and never loses you. "Blood Brothers" is the closest Harris has written to a power ballad and it is not soft, it is not sentimental, it is a song about mortality between men who have been through something together and it is devastating. "Dream of Mirrors" is nine minutes and twenty-one seconds and it earns every one of them.
>
> *Dance of Death* gave us "Paschendale" — the Great War as Harris sees it, which is to say with a level of historical detail and emotional commitment that most war films don't manage. "Rainmaker" is the hit and it's gorgeous, Dickinson's voice soaring over one of Murray's finest melodies. "Journeyman" closes the album acoustically, which is a thing Iron Maiden are not supposed to do, and it is quietly remarkable.
>
> *A Matter of Life and Death* is the most relentlessly heavy album they ever made. They played it front to back on tour and the crowd stood for every second. "For the Greater Good of God" is nine and a half minutes of theological wrestling. "The Legacy" closes the album with an elegiac beauty that Harris has no business possessing and yet clearly does.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume VI: The Endless Frontier (2010–2021)

*Double albums, 18-minute epics, and the refusal to stop.*

> Here is the thing that defies explanation. Iron Maiden, in their sixth decade, started writing double albums. Not as a vanity exercise, not as a contractual obligation, but because Harris had too much to say and refused to edit. *The Final Frontier*, *The Book of Souls*, and *Senjutsu* are, between them, nearly four hours of new music from a band whose members are approaching seventy, and the extraordinary thing — the thing that makes this entire anthology necessary — is that the quality did not drop.
>
> "When the Wild Wind Blows" closes *The Final Frontier* at nearly eleven minutes and it is Harris at his narrative best — a story about an elderly couple convinced the world is ending, based on the Raymond Briggs graphic novel, and it builds to a conclusion of terrible, quiet sadness. "Empire of the Clouds" on *The Book of Souls* is eighteen minutes and one second about the R101 airship disaster, composed by Dickinson on piano, and it is the most audacious thing this band has ever attempted, which given the catalogue we are discussing is genuinely saying something.
>
> *Senjutsu* arrived in 2021 and "Hell on Earth" — eleven minutes, nineteen seconds, the final track — is one of the great closers in the history of rock music: a meditation on conflict, suffering, and the persistence of hope that builds through multiple movements to an ending of exhausted, hard-won beauty. "Darkest Hour" is Dickinson writing about Churchill's war cabinet and making it feel like yesterday. "The Parchment" is twelve minutes about the Dead Sea Scrolls.
>
> These are men in their sixties writing twelve-minute songs about the Dead Sea Scrolls. They do not care if you think it's ridiculous. They are right not to care.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Live Appendix: Up the Irons

*Live After Death, Rock in Rio, and the sound of 250,000 people losing their minds.*

> I have seen a great many live recordings in my time. I have seen the Stones at Altamont and the Pistols at the 100 Club and Iggy with the Stooges at Max's Kansas City. None of them — not one — prepared me for what happens when Iron Maiden play "Fear of the Dark" to a hundred thousand Brazilians. The call-and-response on that chorus is not a concert, it is a seismic event. It is the sound of rock and roll functioning as religion, which is what it was always supposed to be and so rarely actually is.
>
> *Live After Death* from 1985 is the definitive document of the *Powerslave* era — Churchill's speech, then "Aces High," then two hours of controlled devastation. "Hallowed Be Thy Name" live is the best version of the song that exists. *Rock in Rio* from 2001 captured the reunion at its most euphoric — "The Wicker Man" opening to a quarter of a million people, "Blood Brothers" making grown men weep, and "Sign of the Cross" proving that even a Blaze-era song becomes something transcendent in Dickinson's hands.
>
> The live appendix here draws from across the decades. Every one of these recordings is the sound of a band that treats every show as though it matters, which it does, because for the kid in the fifteenth row who saved up for three months to be there, it is the most important night of their life. Harris has never forgotten that. It's why the band endures.
>
> — *N.K.*

*They were doing precisely what we always claimed to want from rock music. They were taking it seriously.*
