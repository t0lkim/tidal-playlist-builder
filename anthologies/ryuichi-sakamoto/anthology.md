# Ryuichi Sakamoto: An Anthology

## *"Ars Longa, Vita Brevis"* — Six Volumes and an Appendix Spanning Forty-Five Years of Sound

---

### Liner Notes

*by Hua Hsu, for The New Yorker*

---

There was a used-CD store on Telegraph Avenue where I spent most of my junior year. It was the kind of place that filed Yellow Magic Orchestra under "World Music," which tells you everything you need to know about how Americans understood Japanese electronic music in 1999. I found a copy of *BTTB* there, in the wrong section, filed between a Yanni compilation and a didgeridoo album. The clerk didn't know who Sakamoto was. "Is this, like, spa music?" he asked. I said I didn't know. I wasn't sure I did.

I bought it because of the cover — a man's hands on a piano, shot from above, the fingers slightly blurred as though the photograph had caught them in the middle of deciding what to play. I listened to it in my car in the parking lot with the engine off, and "Energy Flow" came on, and I sat there for the duration of the track not doing anything at all. Not thinking about the essay I had due. Not checking the time. Just sitting in a Honda Civic on Telegraph Avenue while a Japanese composer I'd never heard of played a melody so simple it felt like something I had always known but never been able to hum.

That was the beginning, though I didn't know it yet. You never know the beginning when it's happening. You only recognise it later, after the thing it started has become part of the architecture of your life — so embedded you can't remember what the rooms looked like before it arrived.

Ryuichi Sakamoto's career is the kind that resists summary because it resists the premise of summary — the idea that a life's work can be described in terms of a direction. He did not go from electronic to classical, or from pop to ambient, or from commercial to experimental. He did all of these things at once, in parallel, often contradicting himself from one album to the next, and the contradictions were not confusion. They were the point. He was interested in what happened when you placed things next to each other that didn't belong together — a Debussy étude and an 808 kick, an Okinawan folk song and a Brian Wilson harmony, the sound of Arctic ice melting and the sound of a piano string vibrating into silence. He was interested in whether "belonging together" was a real category or merely a habit of listening.

He was born in Tokyo in 1952 and he died in Tokyo in 2023, and between those two facts he co-founded Yellow Magic Orchestra, won an Academy Award, composed the most recognisable film theme of the 1980s, collaborated with everyone from David Bowie to Alva Noto, became one of the most visible anti-nuclear activists in Japan after Fukushima, and made two albums while dying of cancer that are among the most quietly devastating recordings in the history of composed sound. The word "genius" gets deployed too freely in music writing. I will use it here because I can't think of another word that accounts for the range, the restlessness, and the fact that he kept getting better at making music right up until the point where his body could no longer sit at the piano.

What follows is an attempt to arrange forty-five years of sound into seven volumes. It is, necessarily, incomplete. Sakamoto's catalogue is too vast and too varied for any single anthology to contain. Think of it as a listening path rather than a definitive statement — one possible route through a landscape that rewards exploration in every direction.

I wish I could tell you how it ends. But you already know. It ends with twelve short pieces, each named after the date it was composed, made by a man who knew he was dying and who chose to spend his remaining days doing the only thing that had ever made complete sense to him. It ends at a piano, as it began.

— *H.H., Brooklyn, 2026*

## Volume I: Electric Youth (1978–1983)

*Yellow Magic Orchestra and the birth of electronic pop. Thousand Knives to Merry Christmas Mr. Lawrence.*

> I have a theory — unverifiable, probably wrong, but I'll state it anyway — that there is a particular kind of joy that only exists in recordings made by people who have just invented something and don't yet know what it is. You can hear it on the first Ramones album, on Kraftwerk's *Autobahn*, on Donna Summer's "I Feel Love." And you can hear it on Yellow Magic Orchestra's *Solid State Survivor*, in the way "Technopolis" announces itself like a city being switched on for the first time, all neon and circuitry and a future that hasn't happened yet but is about to.
>
> Sakamoto was twenty-six when YMO released their debut in 1978. He had a master's degree in electronic and ethnic music from the Tokyo University of the Arts. Hosono and Takahashi were already experienced musicians. The three of them understood, with a precision that bordered on prophecy, that the synthesiser was not merely an instrument but a proposition — that if you could program a machine to make music, you were also asking what music was for. "Behind the Mask" was a pop song played through a vocoder, which meant it was also a question about whether the voice needed to be human to be expressive. Michael Jackson would answer that question a decade later by covering it.
>
> Sakamoto's solo debut, *Thousand Knives*, came out the same year as YMO's first album, and it already sounded like the work of someone who had outgrown the band he had just formed. "Riot in Lagos," from *B-2 Unit* two years later, is often cited as a blueprint for electro, for techno, for the entire species of machine-made funk that would emerge in Detroit and Chicago and Berlin throughout the 1980s. Sakamoto made it in London with a Jamaican dub engineer. The categories hadn't been invented yet. He didn't need them.
>
> — *H.H.*

## Volume II: The World in a Wire (1984–1989)

*Left Handed Dream through Beauty. Seven solo albums in six years, each a different continent.*

> Here is what I find extraordinary about the 1980s solo albums, considered as a single body of work: each one sounds like it was made by a different person. *Ongaku Zukan* is an illustrated encyclopaedia of world sounds. *Esperanto* is pointy modernist architecture rendered in audio. *Futurista* is an art-history seminar disguised as a pop record. *Neo Geo* is Iggy Pop and Bootsy Collins and Tony Williams and a Chinese pipa player in the same studio, which should not work and absolutely does. *Beauty* is a Rolling Stones cover sitting next to an Okinawan folk song, and neither one blinks.
>
> I once asked a friend — a composer, someone who had studied Sakamoto's work more carefully than I had — what connected these albums. She thought about it for a long time. "Curiosity," she said finally. "Just enormous, ungovernable curiosity." That seemed right. Sakamoto had the kind of restlessness that other artists call reinvention but that in his case was something closer to attention — a genuine inability to stop noticing that the world contained more sounds than any single aesthetic could accommodate.
>
> He was living between Tokyo and New York by then, embedded in the downtown art scene, collaborating with Arto Lindsay and Bill Laswell, visiting Nam June Paik's studio, collecting field recordings. The Academy Award for *The Last Emperor* came in 1988, and suddenly the most experimental musician in Japan was also an Oscar-winning film composer. He did not see a contradiction. "I try not to repeat what I've done in the past," he said once, and you could hear in the flatness of the statement that he meant it literally. Each album was an experiment, and when the experiment was done, he moved on.
>
> — *H.H.*

## Volume III: Bibo no Aozora (1991–1998)

*The pop-to-piano transition. Discord, BTTB, and the first instrumental number one in Japan.*

> The nineties were when Sakamoto stopped performing the idea of Sakamoto. Or maybe he stopped performing altogether and just started composing. The distinction matters. A performer occupies a stage; a composer occupies a question.
>
> *Heartbeat* still had the collaborators, the guest vocalists, the world-music production values. *Sweet Revenge* still had the pop hooks. But something was shifting underneath, tectonic and slow, and you could hear it most clearly on "Bibo no Aozora" — a piano piece so gentle it felt less like music and more like weather, like something that had always been happening in the background and you had only just now noticed. The title means "blue of the azure sky." Sakamoto wrote it as a three-minute composition. Alejandro González Iñárritu would later use it to close *Babel*, and hearing it over those final images of disconnected people trying to reach each other across vast distances, you understood that this was what the piece had always been about. It just needed a film to prove it.
>
> *Discord* was an hour-long orchestral work about Africa. *BTTB* was solo piano influenced by Satie. Between them, in the space of one year, Sakamoto demonstrated that he contained at least two completely different composers, and that both of them were serious. "Energy Flow," from *BTTB*, became a number-one single in Japan — the first instrumental ever to do so. A nation of a hundred and twenty-five million people put a solo piano piece at the top of their charts. I think about that sometimes. I think about what it means when a country collectively decides it needs to hear a piano and nothing else.
>
> — *H.H.*

## Volume IV: A Thousand Screens (1987–2023)

*Film scores. The Last Emperor to Monster — forty years of cinema as compositional canvas.*

> Sakamoto once described film scoring as "serving the director's vision," and then he would go and compose something that transcended the film entirely, which is either a contradiction or the highest possible form of service. The "Merry Christmas Mr. Lawrence" theme is more famous than the film. "Bibo no Aozora" was a standalone piano piece before it was a *Babel* cue. The *Last Emperor* score won the Oscar and then spent the next four decades being played by piano students who had never seen the film and never would. His music kept escaping the frames it was composed for, which is what happens when you put a genuinely original musical mind inside the constraints of narrative cinema and ask it to behave.
>
> What strikes me most about the film work, taken as a whole, is the arc from lushness to austerity. The *Last Emperor* is richly orchestral, layered, gorgeous. *The Revenant*, nearly thirty years later, is glacial and spare — composed with Alva Noto, his long-time collaborator in digital minimalism, and it sounds less like a film score than like the landscape itself exhaling. *Monster*, his final score for Hirokazu Kore-eda, premiered at Cannes after his death. I have not been able to listen to it without thinking about what it means to compose for a film about hidden truths when you know you are running out of time to tell your own.
>
> — *H.H.*

## Volume V: Noise and Silence (2002–2009)

*Alva Noto, Fennesz, Morelenbaum. The experimental decade — glitch, ambient, Arctic field recordings.*

> This is the period when Sakamoto's music became harder to describe, which I think is when it became most itself. The collaborations with Alva Noto — Carsten Nicolai, the German sound artist — reduced composition to its atomic elements: a piano note, a sine wave, the space between them. *Vrioon* was voted Record of the Year by *The Wire* in 2004. Five people bought it. I exaggerate, but not by much. The audience for this music was small and it was devoted and it understood something that the mainstream had not yet grasped: that Sakamoto was not retreating from pop into obscurity. He was advancing toward something.
>
> *Chasm* was the bridge. It had David Sylvian singing "World Citizen" — one of the great Sakamoto vocal tracks, earnest and wounded and political in a way that felt personal rather than declamatory — alongside noise experiments and ambient passages that went nowhere and were better for it. *Out of Noise* took him to the Arctic, where he recorded the sound of glaciers and incorporated them into compositions so quiet you had to turn the volume up to hear them and then, when you did, you realised the quietness was the whole point.
>
> I keep coming back to something he said in an interview: "I want to have less notes and more spaces. Spaces, not silence. Space is resonant, is still ringing." That distinction — between silence and space — is the key to everything he did after the year 2000. Silence is nothing. Space is everything that remains after you stop playing.
>
> — *H.H.*

## Volume VI: Ars Longa, Vita Brevis (2017–2023)

*async and 12. The final works, composed in the awareness of death.*

> He was diagnosed with throat cancer in 2014 and he did not make music for a year. This is the fact that reorganises everything. A man who had made music continuously for thirty-six years stopped, and in the stopping he discovered what music had been for him all along — not expression, not communication, not art in any of the ways we usually mean the word, but something closer to breathing. When he started again, it sounded different. Not because his technique had changed but because his relationship to time had.
>
> *async* is the album I return to most often, and I'm not sure I can tell you why without sounding like I'm overselling it. It was conceived as the soundtrack to an Andrei Tarkovsky film that doesn't exist. It incorporates field recordings, out-of-tune pianos, synthesiser drones, spoken word from David Sylvian and Paul Bowles, city noise, and long passages of near-silence. It is not pleasant. It is not trying to be. It is trying to be true to the experience of a person who has recently been very close to death and has returned with a different understanding of what sounds are worth making.
>
> And then *12*. His final album, released on his seventy-first birthday, two months before he died. Twelve pieces, each named after the date it was composed — "20210310," "20220123," "20220302." No titles, no narratives, no programme notes. Just dates. The diary of a man sitting at a piano and a synthesiser, recording whatever came out, not because it would be heard but because the act of making sound was itself a form of remaining alive. "I had no intention of composing something," he said. "I just wanted to be showered in sound."
>
> I listened to *12* the day it came out, in January 2023, not knowing he would be dead by March. I listened to it again the week he died, and it sounded completely different — not because the music had changed but because I had, because knowledge of an ending transforms everything that precedes it. There is a track called "20220302" that is almost unbearably beautiful. It lasts three minutes. Three minutes of piano and faint electronic haze and what might be breathing and what might be the sound of a room with a person in it. I don't know what else to say about it. I don't think saying more would help.
>
> — *H.H.*

## Appendix: The Conversations

*Sylvian, Alva Noto, Fennesz, Morelenbaum. The collaborative life as artistic practice.*

> Sakamoto once said he deliberately sought collaborators with "fundamentally different skills and perspectives" and avoided working with people who shared his imagination. This is unusual. Most musicians collaborate to amplify what they already do. Sakamoto collaborated to become someone he wasn't yet. Each partnership was a form of travel — not geographical but perceptual, a way of hearing through someone else's ears.
>
> David Sylvian was the first and longest conversation — from "Bamboo Houses" in 1982 to "World Citizen" in 2004, a span of twenty-two years interrupted by long silences that only made the reunions more resonant. Sylvian's voice — that impossibly elegant, slightly wounded instrument — gave Sakamoto's compositions an emotional specificity they didn't have on their own. "Forbidden Colours" is, I think, the most beautiful thing either of them ever made, and it required both of them to exist.
>
> The Alva Noto partnership was more architectural — five albums spelling V-I-R-U-S, each one an exercise in restraint, in seeing how little was needed for music to happen. The Fennesz collaborations sent piano recordings across the Atlantic to be layered with guitar and electronics in Vienna. The Morelenbaum sessions were recorded in Antonio Carlos Jobim's house above Rio de Janeiro, because of course they were — because Sakamoto understood that music is also made by the room it happens in, by the ghosts of the people who lived there before you.
>
> I think of these collaborations as conversations in the old sense — not exchanges of information but acts of mutual attention. Sakamoto listened to what his collaborators heard, and what his collaborators heard changed what he was able to play. That's not compromise. That's how you spend forty-five years never making the same record twice.
>
> — *H.H.*

*An anthology compiled in the spring of 2026, three years after his death, by someone who first heard his music in a parking lot on Telegraph Avenue and spent the next twenty-seven years trying to explain why it mattered. It mattered because it was the sound of a person thinking. That is always enough.*
