# New Model Army: The Complete Anthology

## *"No Rest for the Righteous"* — Six Volumes Spanning Forty-Four Years of Unbroken Fury

---

### Liner Notes

*by Nick Kent, for the NME*

---

Let's get one thing absolutely straight before we go any further. While every glib little scenester from the Bromley contingent to the Camden toilet circuit was busy reinventing themselves for the next press cycle — while the so-called arbiters of British taste were falling over each other to crown whichever photogenic non-entity happened to be wearing the right trousers that particular Tuesday — there was a band in Bradford who simply did not care. Did not care about your approval, did not care about your scene, did not care about your wretched little hierarchies of cool. And that, right there, is why New Model Army matter more than any of them. More than the lot of them put together, in fact.

Justin Sullivan formed this band in 1980 and he is still — *still* — fronting it in 2024, which makes him either a visionary or a lunatic and I'd wager good money he's magnificently both. The music press never knew what to do with NMA. Too punk for the folkies, too folk for the punks, too Northern for London, too literate for the lager crowd, too angry for the broadsheets, too earnest for the inkies. They fell between every stool the British music establishment had carefully arranged, and in doing so they built something those stool-arrangers could never manufacture: a following that would walk through fire. And I don't use that phrase lightly.

Robert Heaton sat behind the drums from 1982 until his body wouldn't let him anymore. He died in November 2004 of pancreatic cancer, and if you listen to "Fireworks Night" on the *Carnival* album without something catching in your throat then you are, frankly, dead inside and beyond the reach of rock and roll. Heaton was the rhythmic backbone of everything from *Vengeance* through *Strange Brotherhood* — sixteen years of relentless, tribal, utterly committed drumming. His death didn't kill the band. Nothing kills this band. That's rather the point.

What you hold in your hands is six volumes spanning forty-four years. Six volumes that trace an arc from the raw, spitting fury of Bradford post-punk through to the orchestral grandeur of *Sinfonia* with the Leipzig Orchestra. It is, I would argue, one of the great unsung catalogues in British rock — and the fact that it remains unsung tells you everything you need to know about the catastrophic failure of taste that has defined mainstream British music criticism for four decades.

Start anywhere. Start with "Vagabonds" if you want the anthem. Start with "Vengeance" if you want the manifesto. Start with "Green And Grey" if you want your heart broken. Start with "51st State" if you want to understand why America makes us nervous. Start with "Born Feral" if you want proof that a band can get *better* after thirty-five years, which is a thing that simply does not happen except when it does.

This is the real thing. No posturing, no reinvention, no career pivots into electronica or bloody trip-hop. Just a band from Bradford who decided what they were in 1980 and then spent the next four and a half decades becoming more of it.

The rest of them can go hang.

— *N.K., London, 2026*

## Volume I: The Bradford Fire (1980–1984)

*Raw post-punk fury from the independent years. Seven days in Alaska Studios, Waterloo. The manifesto.*

> The *Vengeance* album was recorded in seven days at Alaska Studios in Waterloo for what I'm reliably informed was about four hundred quid. Seven days. Four hundred quid. And it sounds like a building collapsing — which is precisely what Sullivan intended, because the building in question was the entire rotten edifice of Thatcher's Britain and he wanted you to hear every brick hit the ground. "Smalltown England" is the best song about provincial claustrophobia since the Kinks stopped bothering, and "Spirit of the Falklands" was the kind of thing that got you put on Special Branch watch lists in 1984. Sullivan didn't flinch. He never flinches. The Acoustic EP tracks at the end — "Adrenalin," "Nosense," "Trust" — are included here because they prove something the leather-jacket contingent never understood: this band could strip back to nothing and still draw blood.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume II: The State of the Nation (1985–1987)

*The EMI years. Political fire, banned from Top of the Pops, and the song that named a generation's rage.*

> So EMI signs them. EMI — the label of the Beatles, the Stones, the Sex Pistols for about five minutes — signs New Model Army, and you can practically hear the A&R men in Manchester Square congratulating themselves on having discovered authenticity. What they actually discovered was a band that would not be managed, would not be styled, and would absolutely not shut up about politics on *Top of the Pops*. Sullivan wore a shirt reading "Only Stupid Bastards Use Heroin" on the show and the BBC, in its infinite wisdom, banned them. Banned them! This was, naturally, the best thing that could have happened.
>
> *No Rest for the Wicked* is a furious record and *The Ghost of Cain* is a great one. "51st State" remains the single most devastating critique of American cultural imperialism ever set to a three-chord riff — and I include everything the Clash ever recorded in that assessment, which I realise is fighting talk but I don't care and neither did Sullivan. "Poison Street" is about heroin and it is merciless and it is true. "The Hunt" is about Thatcher's police state and it is merciless and it is true. The B-sides here — "Ten Commandments," "Courage" — are better than most bands' A-sides, which tells you everything about the depth of the catalogue we're dealing with.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume III: Thunder (1989–1991)

*The masterwork period. Folk, punk, and rock in perfect collision. The anthems. The fury. The live devastation of Raw Melody Men.*

> Right. Here it is. The peak. The absolute summit. If you own one New Model Army record it will be *Thunder and Consolation* and you will be right to own it because it is, track for track, one of the finest rock albums ever made in this country and I will fight anyone who says otherwise.
>
> "Vagabonds" is the anthem. You know this already. Every festival crowd from Glastonbury to some freezing field in Germany has bellowed it back at Sullivan with a fervour that borders on the religious, and that is not a coincidence because Sullivan writes hymns — he just writes them for people who've been thrown out of the church. "Green And Grey" is the most beautiful song about England since "Waterloo Sunset," a thing of devastating simplicity and quiet, aching patriotism that has nothing to do with flags and everything to do with landscape and loss. "225" hits like a freight train. "Stupid Questions" is punk rock played by people who actually have something to say.
>
> And then *Impurity* — darker, harder, the hangover after the party — followed by the B-sides. "Deadeye." "Ghost of Your Father." These are songs that other bands would have killed to have as album tracks, tossed away on seven-inch singles that sold to the converted and nobody else. The waste of it. The magnificent, stubborn waste.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume IV: The Wilderness (1993–1998)

*EMI drops them. Heaton's health fails. The music gets harder, then more reflective. Defiant to the last.*

> EMI dropped them in 1993 because EMI are, and have always been, cowards. *The Love of Hopeless Causes* is the sound of a band responding to corporate betrayal by getting angrier and more focused, which is not the response the music industry expects and not the response it knows how to deal with. "Here Comes The War" opens like a bomb going off and it doesn't stop. "Living In The Rose" is Sullivan at his most lyrically precise — a love song that isn't soft, a political song that isn't hectoring, a rock song that isn't stupid.
>
> And then silence. Five years of it. Heaton's health was failing, though we didn't know how badly. When *Strange Brotherhood* finally appeared in 1998 it was quieter, more reflective, threaded with a melancholy that Sullivan had always carried but never quite let dominate. "Queen Of My Heart" is a ballad and it is gorgeous and if you'd told the spikey-haired crowd at the Adelphi in '84 that this band would one day write something this tender they'd have spat at you, but they'd have been wrong.
>
> The *Lost Songs* material collected here — "Southwest," "Song To The Men Of England," "See You In Hell" — is the sound of a band writing into the void, with no label, no press, no support, and absolutely no intention of stopping.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume V: Ghosts and Carnivals (2000–2009)

*Heaton dies. The band doesn't. Tribal rhythms, atmospheric landscapes, and Sullivan's elegy for a friend.*

> Robert Heaton died on the 4th of November 2004 and I am not going to write about it with any distance or critical detachment because there is no distance to be had and detachment would be obscene. He was forty-three. He had played on every New Model Army album from *Vengeance* to *Strange Brotherhood*. His drumming was the tribal heartbeat underneath everything — not flashy, never flashy, but absolutely relentless and absolutely right.
>
> *Eight* came out in 2000 and it was the sound of the band discovering texture — "Snelsmore Wood" and "Paekakariki Beach" are landscape paintings in sound, Sullivan's voice carrying you through forests and across shorelines with a specificity of place that most songwriters never achieve because most songwriters don't actually *look* at anything. "Leeds Road 3am" is Bradford at night and it is perfect.
>
> Then Heaton dies, and *Carnival* happens, and "Fireworks Night" happens, and if you can listen to that song knowing what you know without something fundamental shifting inside you then I have nothing more to say to you and this anthology is not for you. "BD3" is the Bradford postcode and it is Sullivan's love letter to home and it is devastatingly simple and devastatingly effective. *High* pushed into harder territory — "All Consuming Fire" is exactly what it says — and *Today Is a Good Day* closed the decade with a defiance that by this point should surprise nobody but somehow still does.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Volume VI: The Late Burning (2013–2024)

*The renaissance. Critics finally catch up. The orchestra arrives. Forty-four years in and the fire burns hotter than ever.*

> Here is the thing that isn't supposed to happen. Here is the part of the story where the script says the band should be winding down, playing the nostalgia circuit, trotting out "Vagabonds" and "51st State" to greying crowds who clap politely and go home early. Here is the part where the fire is supposed to go out.
>
> It didn't go out. It got *hotter*.
>
> *Between Dog and Wolf* arrived in 2013 and "Horsemen" is one of the best things Sullivan has ever written — an apocalyptic gallop that sounds like the end of the world viewed from a hilltop in West Yorkshire, which is probably the best place to view it from. "Stormclouds" builds and builds until it breaks over you. *Winter* in 2016 was even better — "Born Feral" is a statement of intent from a band thirty-six years deep that had no business being this vital and was anyway. "Burn The Castle" is punk rock fury from a man in his late fifties and it is magnificent and if you think there's something undignified about that then you have fundamentally misunderstood what rock and roll is for.
>
> *From Here* and *Unbroken* closed the arc — or didn't close it, because Sullivan shows no sign of stopping — with a maturity and power that made the broadsheet critics finally sit up and pay attention, forty years too late. "End Of Days" is eschatological folk-punk. "From Here" is Sullivan looking back at everything and finding it good. "Deserters" is the last word, for now, and it is defiant, and it is beautiful, and it is exactly what you'd expect.
>
> — *N.K.*

## Appendix: The Essential Live Recordings

*For when you need to hear what the Family sounds like in a room together.*

> You cannot understand this band from studio recordings alone. You can admire them, certainly. You can recognise the craft, the fury, the intelligence. But you cannot *understand* them until you have heard them in a room full of the Family — which is what NMA's audience calls itself, and it is not an affectation, it is a simple description of fact.
>
> *Raw Melody Men* from 1991 captures the *Thunder* era live and it is ferocious — "Vagabonds" with two thousand voices singing it back is not a concert, it is a covenant. The *Between Wine and Blood* live tracks catch the later band in full flight, and the *Sinfonia* recordings with the Neue Philharmonie Leipzig are the most audacious thing Sullivan has ever attempted: the entire NMA catalogue reimagined with a full orchestra, and it works, God help us, it actually works. "Green And Grey" with strings is enough to finish you off entirely.
>
> — *N.K.*
