# David Bowie — An Anthology

## As Told by Someone Who Gives a Damn

Look, I'll be straight with you: David Bowie was the most infuriating, magnificent, phony, transcendent, calculated, accidental genius rock and roll ever produced, and if you think you can pin him down with a playlist you're dumber than the record executives who kept trying to market him as one thing. He wasn't one thing. He wasn't even ten things. He was a guy from Bromley with weird eyes who decided the entire history of popular music was his personal costume rack, and the crazy part is HE WAS RIGHT.

So here's what we've done. We've taken fifty-two years of relentless shapeshifting and crammed it into a sequence that makes some kind of sense, which is already a betrayal of everything Bowie stood for, because Bowie didn't make sense. He made MUSIC. Sometimes it was the most gorgeous sound you ever heard in your life. Sometimes it was unlistenable art-school garbage. Sometimes — and this is the part that'll keep you up at night — it was both at the same time.

The famous songs are here because they have to be. But the famous songs are NOT the point. The point is the songs between the famous songs — the seven-minute nightmares, the B-sides that got thrown away, the album tracks that three hundred people on earth have strong opinions about. Those are the songs where Bowie forgot he was supposed to be David Bowie and just played. Those are the songs that'll rearrange your skull.

If you know every track in this collection, congratulations, you're a maniac and I love you. If you discover one song that makes you pull your headphones off and go "what the hell was THAT" — well, that's rock and roll, baby. That's what it's for.

---

## Volume I: The Bewildered Boy (1964-1969)

Before any of the nonsense — before the spacesuits and the makeup and the fascist salutes from the back of a convertible — there was just this KID. This weird, hungry kid from the London suburbs who wanted to be famous so badly it was practically coming out of his pores like flop sweat. And the early records are a MESS. They're mod, they're music hall, they're Anthony Newley doing a bad impression of Anthony Newley, they're mime (MIME! In 1967!), and every once in a while there's a flash of something so raw and so real it makes your teeth hurt.

**The London Boys** is the first great David Bowie song and I will fight anyone who says otherwise. He was NINETEEN. Nineteen years old and he wrote a song about a kid getting chewed up by the London pill scene that's got more genuine heartbreak in it than most people manage in an entire career. The melody is devastating. The arrangement is devastating. Decca Records took one look at it and buried it as a B-side because it was too dark. Too DARK. This is the music industry, folks. They wouldn't know greatness if it bit them on the balance sheet.

**We Are Hungry Men** is completely nuts. It's a satirical monologue about overpopulation and eugenics delivered by a twenty-year-old who clearly thought he was Jonathan Swift. It's also terrible. It's also brilliant. It's the kind of song only someone with no self-awareness and total genius would attempt, and the fact that Bowie was both of those things simultaneously is basically the whole story of his career right there.

Then **Space Oddity**. Five days before Apollo 11. The BBC played it over the moon landing footage, which is hilarious because the song is about an astronaut WHO DIES. Nobody in the control room thought to actually LISTEN to the lyrics. It made him famous for about fifteen minutes and then he spent the next three years eating toast and wondering if he'd blown his only shot.

But here's the thing nobody tells you about the *Space Oddity* album: IT'S FULL OF MASTERPIECES. **Cygnet Committee** is nine minutes of absolute fury about the death of the hippie dream — this is 1969, when everyone else was still painting flowers on their vans, and Bowie was already writing the autopsy report. **Letter to Hermione** is the most naked, vulnerable thing he ever recorded: a man singing to a woman who left him, with nothing to hide behind. No persona. No costume. Just a guy with a guitar and a broken heart. It's almost unbearable. **Wild Eyed Boy from Freecloud** is a gothic hallucination that sounds like it was beamed in from some other, better planet. These are not the songs of a novelty act. These are the songs of someone who was going to eat the world alive.

## Volume II: The Man Who Sold the World (1970-1971)

So Bowie grew his hair out, put on a dress for the album cover — an ACTUAL DRESS, this was 1970, people were LOSING THEIR MINDS — and made a heavy metal record with a guitarist named Mick Ronson who could make a Les Paul sound like a chainsaw cutting through a cathedral. Nobody bought it. It was incredible.

**The Width of a Circle** kicks off with eight minutes of guitar-driven PSYCHOSIS. The lyrics are some unholy marriage of Nietzsche and Aleister Crowley, the riff has more horsepower than a 427 Cobra, and Ronson's guitar solo — Lord have mercy. I've heard people compare it to Hendrix and I've heard people compare it to Sabbath and they're both wrong. It doesn't sound like anyone else. It sounds like a man who has decided, consciously and deliberately, to become DANGEROUS.

**All the Madmen** — here's where it gets heavy. Bowie's half-brother Terry was locked up in Cane Hill psychiatric hospital, and this song is about him, and the line "I'd rather stay here with all the madmen than perish with the sad men roaming free" is NOT A METAPHOR. Bowie meant it. Terry's shadow falls across everything Bowie ever wrote, and if you don't understand that, you don't understand Bowie.

**She Shook Me Cold** is heavier than anything Black Sabbath was doing that year and I'm including *Paranoid* in that assessment. **Saviour Machine** is about a computer that gets bored of saving humanity and starts manufacturing crises instead, which in 1970 was science fiction and in 2026 is the evening news.

## Volume III: Ziggy Stardust & the Spiders (1971-1973)

Alright. This is it. This is the part where David Bowie stops being an interesting failure and becomes the single most important performer in the history of rock and roll. Three albums in two years: *Hunky Dory*, *Ziggy Stardust*, *Aladdin Sane*. Each one a masterpiece. Each one sounds nothing like the other two. Most artists would kill for ONE of these records. Bowie knocked out three like he was flicking cigarette butts off a rooftop.

**Life on Mars?** started as a SPITE PROJECT. Bowie wanted to write the English lyrics for a French pop song called "Comme d'habitude" — the one that became "My Way" — and when they gave the gig to Paul Anka instead, Bowie sat down and wrote a BETTER SONG out of pure wounded pride. Same chord progression, entirely different universe. Rick Wakeman played the piano part in one take. ONE TAKE. And it's one of the most beautiful pieces of music in the history of Western civilisation. That's spite for you. Spite is the great motivator.

**Quicksand** is the closest Bowie ever came to telling you what was actually going on inside his head: "I'm not a prophet or a stone-age man, just a mortal with potential of a superman." That's it. That's the whole Bowie project in one sentence. The rest of the career is just footnotes. **The Bewlay Brothers** is the one even Bowie couldn't explain. He said it was "the first song I've written that I haven't been able to explain." Most people think it's about Terry. Most people are probably right.

On *Ziggy*, everybody knows "Starman" and "Suffragette City" and "Ziggy Stardust" itself, but LISTEN to **Soul Love**. Nobody listens to Soul Love. It's the track everyone skips between "Five Years" and "Moonage Daydream" and it's a jazzy little miracle about different kinds of love and it's the song that separates the tourists from the devotees.

**Velvet Goldmine** got THROWN OFF the album for being too sexually explicit. FOR ZIGGY STARDUST. The album about an omnisexual alien rock star. Too explicit. It's a glam-rock stomp about oral sex and it's magnificent and it ended up as a B-side in 1975 and gave its name to a Todd Haynes movie.

**All the Young Dudes** — Bowie wrote this for Mott the Hoople, who were about to break up because nobody was buying their records. He offered them "Suffragette City" first. They said no. So he went HOME and wrote them a BETTER SONG OVERNIGHT. Just — here, have a career-saving anthem, I whipped it up before breakfast. Bowie's own demo is rawer and more personal than the Mott version and I'll listen to either one any day of the week.

**Lady Grinning Soul** is the most underrated song in the entire Bowie catalogue and I will go to my grave saying this. Mike Garson's piano — half Chopin, half cocktail lounge, all genius — and Bowie singing about a woman named Claudia Lennear (one of Ike Turner's Ikettes, possibly the Brown Sugar) with this voice of barely contained WANT. It closes *Aladdin Sane* and it closes it PERFECTLY.

**Port of Amsterdam** — Bowie doing Jacques Brel. On the BBC. LIVE. He is spitting. He is howling. He is channelling the theatrical fury of a Belgian cabaret singer and making it sound like his own personal demon. It was supposed to close the Ziggy album. Instead they went with "Rock 'n' Roll Suicide." Both would have worked. But this one is FERAL.

## Volume IV: The Diamond Duke (1974-1976)

Three personas in three years and they were all falling apart. Halloween Jack on *Diamond Dogs* — post-apocalyptic street urchin. The blue-eyed soul man on *Young Americans* — philly falsetto. The Thin White Duke on *Station to Station* — cocaine skeleton doing European cabaret while the FBI reportedly surveilled his house. This is the period where Bowie was most obviously, most dangerously, most thrillingly out of his mind.

**Sweet Thing / Candidate / Sweet Thing (Reprise)** is nine minutes of seedy urban noir that Bowie originally wrote for a stage musical of *1984* until George Orwell's estate told him to get lost. So he scrapped the musical and kept the songs and the songs are BETTER for it — homeless and wild and covered in broken glass. The sax is Bowie's own. The guitar is by a session player who was SEVENTEEN YEARS OLD.

**Who Can I Be Now?** sat in a vault for FORTY-ONE YEARS. Forty-one! Recorded during the *Young Americans* sessions, shelved, forgotten, and when it finally came out in 2016 people lost their minds because it's one of the best things from those sessions. A soaring, string-laden meditation on identity. In a vault. For four decades. What ELSE is in there?

**Station to Station** the song. Ten minutes. Starts with the sound of a train. Ends somewhere in the Kabbalistic mysteries of the Sephiroth. Bowie said he doesn't remember recording it. He was living on red peppers, cocaine, and milk. His body was a crime scene. But the MUSIC — the music remembers everything. When that motorik intro breaks open into the guitar-driven second half, it is one of the most transcendent moments in all of rock and roll and I don't care how hyperbolic that sounds because I MEAN IT.

**Stay** never became a hit and that's a CRIME. Six minutes of Earl Slick's wah-wah guitar doing things that should require a permit. **Wild Is the Wind** — Nina Simone did it. Johnny Mathis did it. And then Bowie did it, and people started saying it was the greatest vocal performance of his career, and they might be right. They just might be right.

## Volume V: The Berlin Trilogy (1977-1979)

He ran. He ran from LA, from cocaine, from fame, from himself, and he landed in Berlin — West Berlin, divided Berlin, the city with a wall through its heart — and he made three albums with Brian Eno that broke music in half and put it back together wrong. *Low* and *"Heroes"* are two of the ten greatest albums ever made. I'm not ranking them. I'm stating fact. *Lodger* is the one the critics still can't figure out, which probably means it's the most important of the three.

**Sound and Vision** is pop perfection — under three minutes, shimmering like sunlight on broken glass. Bowie wrote the melody IN THE BATHROOM while Eno and Visconti built the backing track in the studio. The vocal doesn't come in until HALFWAY THROUGH. Number three in the UK for three weeks. Proof that you can reinvent yourself as an avant-garde European art weirdo AND still write a hit.

**Warszawa**. Ten minutes. Ambient. Bowie singing in a language that DOESN'T EXIST — he made it up, sitting in the studio, channelling the feeling of driving from East Berlin to Warsaw through a landscape of grey. It sounds like the death of Europe. It sounds like the birth of something else. Eno's opening four-note motif has haunted every ambient record made since.

**"Heroes"** — I know. You know. Everyone knows. But do you know HOW it was recorded? Hansa Studios, right next to the Wall. Bowie watching a couple kiss at the base of the Wall every day — lovers meeting despite the guns. Robert Fripp's guitar through three microphones. Bowie's vocal through three microphones at INCREASING DISTANCES, each mic gated to open only when he sang LOUDER. So to reach the final mic, he had to SCREAM. That scream — "we can be heroes, JUST FOR ONE DAY" — that scream is the sound of a human being insisting on joy in the face of totalitarian machinery, and if it doesn't make you feel something then check your pulse because you might be dead.

**African Night Flight** from *Lodger* — Bowie reading the lyrics for the first time, in a single take, stumbling and breathless and ecstatic. Eno's concept: "world music made by people who have never been there." **Boys Keep Swinging** — the musicians SWAPPED INSTRUMENTS. Dennis Davis on bass. Carlos Alomar on drums. The whole thing lurches like a drunk. Intentionally. Gloriously.

## Volume VI: The Pop Star (1980-1984)

**Scary Monsters** is the last great Bowie album for a decade — New Wave energy, Berlin brains, pop hooks that could cut steel. Then he made *Let's Dance* and became the biggest pop star on the planet and you could watch the light go out of his eyes in real time.

But before that: **Teenage Wildlife**. Seven minutes. Soaring. Allegedly a middle finger to Gary Numan, who Bowie thought was strip-mining his Berlin sound. Whether or not that's true, the song BURNS. The guitar solo is credited to Robert Fripp and disputed by literally everyone.

**It's No Game (Pt. 1)** opens *Scary Monsters* with a Japanese actress SCREAMING a translation of the lyrics over a wall of distorted guitar. It's the most confrontational thing on an album full of confrontational things. The companion, **Pt. 2**, closes the album as a WHISPER. Same words. Opposite universe.

**Under Pressure** with Queen. Neither Bowie nor Mercury enjoyed making it. Both were control freaks. The result transcended them both and everyone knows it and the bass line by John Deacon is probably playing in your head RIGHT NOW.

**Cat People** — "putting out fire with gasoline." Bowie at his most commanding. Giorgio Moroder at his most pulsing. Tarantino put it in *Inglourious Basterds* and it was the best thing in the movie, which is saying something because that movie is excellent.

**This Is Not America** with Pat Metheny is the opposite of everything else here: quiet, restrained, melancholy. A song about patriotism that manages to be neither celebration nor protest but something sadder and more honest than either. It's the sound of someone who loves a country he no longer recognises.

## Volume VII: The Wilderness Years (1987-1991)

Nobody wants to talk about this part. The *Tonight* album. *Never Let Me Down*. Tin Machine. This is the section of the career that biographers rush through, that fans pretend didn't happen, that critics use as evidence that the Berlin trilogy was a fluke. They're wrong. They're wrong because you don't get to the nineties without going through the wilderness, and Bowie KNEW that. He had to get lost. He had to make bad records. He had to fail so completely and so publicly that when he came back, nobody was watching anymore, and he could finally work in peace.

**Loving the Alien** is the one great song from *Tonight* — seven minutes of synth grandeur about the Crusades that belonged on a completely different album. It's stranded in a sea of Phil Collins-era production choices and it DESERVES BETTER.

**Time Will Crawl** — Bowie's own favourite from *Never Let Me Down*, which is like saying your favourite meal at a bad restaurant, but the song itself is genuinely good. Nuclear anxiety set to a beat. He re-recorded it in 2008 with strings and it's magnificent.

Tin Machine. The band nobody asked for. Reeves Gabrels on guitar, the Sales brothers on rhythm, and Bowie trying to be a band member instead of a star. Critics HATED it. And you know what? **Heaven's in Here** is seven minutes of blues-rock fury that sounds like Bowie remembered he was supposed to be a ROCK AND ROLL SINGER, not a pop star. **Under the God** is anti-fascist punk played at full speed by men who were angry about something real. **I Can't Read** is chaos and frustration and it's better than anything on the last three Bowie solo records. **Crack City** is the most punk thing Bowie ever did and I'm including "Jean Genie" in that assessment.

## Volume VIII: The Art Chameleon (1993-1999)

The nineties were Bowie's second golden age and NOBODY NOTICED. Four albums. Each one different. Each one brilliant. And the world was too busy buying Oasis records to pay attention.

**Jump They Say** is about Terry. Terry jumped off the roof of Cane Hill hospital in 1985. He killed himself. Bowie's half-brother, the man whose shadow fell across every album since the beginning, was dead. And Bowie made a song about it that sounds like paranoia and surveillance and claustrophobia and guilt, and it reached number nine in the UK, and then everyone forgot about it. We shouldn't have forgotten about it.

**1. Outside** with Eno. A concept album about art-murder in the future. I can hear you laughing. Stop laughing. **The Hearts Filthy Lesson** sounds like a machine having a nightmare. **Thru' These Architects Eyes** is soaring pop perfection with piano solos that arrive like weather. **Strangers When We Meet** is gorgeous in a way that makes you angry because WHY WASN'T THIS A MASSIVE HIT?

**Earthling** — drum and bass. Bowie doing jungle. And it's GOOD, which surprised everyone including, probably, Bowie. **I'm Afraid of Americans** — Trent Reznor remixed it, the video is a paranoid masterpiece, and it's the closest Bowie came to a hit in his late period. **Little Wonder** namedrops all seven dwarfs. Seriously. It works.

**Thursday's Child** opens *hours...* and it's the first time in years that Bowie sounds CALM. Not manic, not performing, not hiding. Just a man sitting with himself, finally, after decades of running.

## Volume IX: The Sage (2002-2004)

Then, out of absolutely nowhere, two of the best albums of his career.

*Heathen*. Recorded after 9/11. You can hear the ash in it. **Sunday** sounds like watching a city burn from orbit. **5:15 the Angels Have Gone** is synthetic choirs and mechanical heartbeats making something genuinely ALIEN. **Heathen (The Rays)** closes the album and Bowie sings it like a man who knows something terrible and beautiful that he can't quite articulate.

*Reality*. **New Killer Star** is the most accessible opener since "Modern Love." **Bring Me the Disco King** — EIGHT MINUTES. Jazz. Mike Garson on piano. The same Garson who played the insane solo on "Aladdin Sane" thirty years prior. Here he plays with a restraint that's almost unbearable, because you know what he's capable of, and he's CHOOSING not to do it. That's artistry. That's the real thing.

Heart attack on stage. 2004. Reality tour. And then: silence. Nine years of it.

## Volume X: The Final Act (2013-2016)

Nobody knew. Nobody had any idea. And then on his sixty-sixth birthday — January 8, 2013 — Bowie dropped **Where Are We Now?** with zero warning and the entire world stopped what it was doing.

*The Next Day* is FURIOUS. This is not a victory lap. This is not an old man being gentle. **The Next Day** is a full-frontal assault on religious hypocrisy. **Valentine's Day** is a love song about a school shooter and the melody is deliberately, sickeningly pretty. **Heat** closes the album in a whisper so quiet you have to lean forward to hear it, like pressing your ear to a door that's closing for the last time.

**Sue (Or in a Season of Crime)** — jazz chaos, operatic in scope, a single released between the two final albums that announced: the farewell will NOT be quiet.

And then **.** Blackstar. Released January 8, 2016. His sixty-ninth birthday. He died two days later.

He KNEW. He knew the whole time he was making it. And he didn't tell anyone, and instead he made this: a ten-minute title track that opens with a solar eclipse and references Major Tom and ISIS and the Book of Revelation and sounds like nothing any human being has ever committed to tape.

**Lazarus**. "Look up here, I'm in heaven." The video: hospital bed, bandages, reaching upward. He's dying and he's TELLING you he's dying and you didn't know until two days later when the news broke and you played it again and — God. GOD.

**Dollar Days**: "If I never see the English evergreens I'm running to, it's nothing to me, it's nothing to see." A man saying goodbye to a country he hasn't lived in for forty years. It is quietly, absolutely annihilating.

**I Can't Give Everything Away** is the last song on the last album. The harmonica at the top is sampled from "A New Career in a New Town" on *Low* — the Berlin album, the album where he was reborn, the album that started everything that mattered. The snake eats its tail. The alien goes home. The boy from Bromley with the weird eyes did something nobody else in the history of this ridiculous art form ever managed: he went out on his own terms, with his best work, and he turned his own death into a masterpiece.

If that doesn't move you, nothing will. And if it does — well, that's rock and roll, baby. That's what it's always been for.

## The Supplement: EPs, Singles & B-sides

An anthology tells one story. The supplement tells the REAL story — the weird, messy, glorious story that happens BETWEEN the albums, in the margins, on the B-sides, in the film soundtracks that nobody saw and the collaborations that nobody asked for. These are the songs that didn't fit the narrative. These are the songs that show you who Bowie was when he wasn't being BOWIE.

Thirty-two tracks. Here's what you need to know about the ones that matter most:

**The Prettiest Star** has Marc Bolan on lead guitar. 1970. They were friends and rivals. Bolan died in a car crash in 1977, two weeks after appearing on Bowie's TV show. **Conversation Piece** is the B-side and it's one of the most nakedly autobiographical songs Bowie ever wrote: a man alone in a bedsit, talking to nobody. I've played it at 3am more times than I should admit.

**John, I'm Only Dancing** was too sexually ambiguous for America. 1972. America wasn't ready. America is never ready.

**Across the Universe** — B-side of "Fame." John Lennon on guitar. Electric Lady Studios. Two of the greatest songwriters of the twentieth century, in the same room, making Beatles covers. The casualness of it is staggering.

The Bing Crosby duet. **Peace on Earth / Little Drummer Boy**. September 1977. Bing died five weeks later. The song became one of the most-played Christmas recordings in history. And you know what? It's genuinely, honestly, no-irony beautiful. Sometimes the universe just gives you something nice.

The **Baal EP** — Bowie doing Brecht for the BBC. 1982. Nobody bought it. The performances are EXTRAORDINARY.

**Dancing in the Street** with Jagger. The video is the worst thing either of them ever did. The single went to number one. Rock and roll makes no sense.

The **Labyrinth** soundtrack. "Magic Dance" is a novelty number with puppets. "Within You" is a genuinely devastating ballad about power and loss. Same movie. Same man. That's Bowie.

The **No Plan EP**. 2017. Posthumous. The last songs. "No Plan" is exactly what it says: a man facing the end without a map, without a scheme, without a character to hide behind. No Ziggy. No Thin White Duke. No Aladdin Sane. Just David. It's the most honest song he ever wrote, and if you can get through it without feeling the floor shift under your feet, you're stronger than I am.

## A Note on What's Missing

Plenty. *Pin Ups* is mostly absent. The full *Buddha of Suburbia* soundtrack. The Iggy Pop collaborations. Tin Machine II isn't on Tidal, which is a platform problem and not a taste problem.

But here's the thing about a Bowie anthology: it CAN'T be complete. The man made too much music, in too many styles, for too many decades. Any attempt at completeness is just another kind of incompleteness. So what we've got here is an invitation. A door.

Walk through it. If you come out the other side wanting more, go find *Buddha of Suburbia*. Go find *The Gouster*. Go listen to Iggy's *The Idiot*, which is a Bowie album wearing an Iggy mask. Go find the BBC sessions, the live albums, the outtakes. There is ALWAYS more Bowie. There will always be more Bowie. That's the gift. That's the impossible, infuriating, magnificent gift.

*186 tracks. 10 volumes. 12 hours. One man who refused, absolutely and completely, to be one thing.*
