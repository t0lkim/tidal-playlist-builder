# David Bowie — An Anthology

## On Curation

This anthology attempts something that David Bowie spent his entire career resisting: a definitive reading of his work. He would have hated it. He would have found the idea of chronological order — of *explaining* him — deeply tedious. But Bowie also understood that the audience completes the art, and this is our completion: a listener's journey through fifty-two years of relentless becoming.

The selection balances the famous with the forgotten. For every "Heroes" there is a "Moss Garden"; for every "Let's Dance" there is a "Shake It". The well-known tracks provide the architecture — the load-bearing walls of each era — while the deep cuts reveal the rooms most visitors never enter. It is in those rooms that Bowie is most himself.

Every track was chosen because it *earns* its place, not because it was a hit. Some of the greatest songs here never charted. Some of the most revelatory moments come from albums the critics dismissed. If you already know every song on this anthology, you are a rare creature indeed, and we salute you. If you discover even one track that changes how you hear Bowie, then this collection has done its work.

## Volume I: The Bewildered Boy (1964–1969)

Before the alien, before the duke, before any of it — there was David Robert Jones, a teenager from Bromley with mismatched pupils and the desperate conviction that he was destined for something extraordinary. The early recordings are a wonderful mess: music hall, mod, mime, Anthony Newley impressions, and the occasional flash of genuine strangeness.

**The London Boys** (1966) is the first great Bowie song, and you could argue he never wrote anything more heartbreaking. A tale of a teenager swallowed by the Soho pill scene, it was too dark for radio and too brilliant to ignore. Decca buried it as a B-side. Bowie was nineteen.

**We Are Hungry Men** (1967) is the kind of song that could only come from a mind unafraid of embarrassment. A satirical sci-fi monologue about overpopulation and totalitarian eugenics — from a 20-year-old — it was wildly misjudged and utterly fascinating. Nobody else would have attempted it.

Then came **Space Oddity** (1969), released five days before Apollo 11. The BBC used it as soundtrack to the moon landing coverage, apparently unconcerned that the song is about an astronaut who *dies*. It made Bowie a one-hit wonder in England and nothing at all in America. He would spend the next three years clawing his way back.

The deep album cuts from the *Space Oddity* LP deserve particular attention. **Cygnet Committee** is a nine-minute epic about the failure of the hippie movement — savage, disillusioned, and years ahead of its time. **Letter to Hermione** is naked vulnerability: Bowie mourning his first serious love, Hermione Farthingale, who left him for a Norwegian dancer. **Wild Eyed Boy from Freecloud** is a gothic fable that Bowie later expanded into a full arrangement with strings, and it contains some of his most evocative lyrical imagery. These are not the songs of a novelty act.

## Volume II: The Man Who Sold the World (1970–1971)

Bowie grew his hair, wore a dress on the album cover (the "man dress" that scandalised America), and made a proto-metal record with Mick Ronson that nobody bought. It was the sound of a man deciding, quite deliberately, to become dangerous.

**The Width of a Circle** opens the album with eight minutes of guitar-driven schizophrenia. The lyrics veer between Nietzsche and Aleister Crowley, the riff could power a small city, and Ronson's guitar solo remains one of the most ferocious ever committed to tape. It was Bowie's declaration that the folk singer with the twelve-string was dead.

**All the Madmen** was written for Bowie's half-brother Terry, who was institutionalised at Cane Hill psychiatric hospital. The line "I'd rather stay here with all the madmen than perish with the sad men roaming free" is not metaphor. Bowie meant it literally. Terry's story would haunt his work for decades.

**She Shook Me Cold** is Black Sabbath-heavy, raw, and utterly unlike anything else Bowie ever recorded. **Saviour Machine** imagines a computer that solves all of humanity's problems, becomes bored, and starts creating crises for entertainment — a premise that feels uncomfortably prescient.

**Holy Holy** exists in two versions: the 1971 single (produced by Herbie Flowers, largely forgotten) and the explosive 1973 re-recording from the Ziggy sessions. The version included here is the Ziggy session take — faster, harder, and infinitely more alive.

## Volume III: Ziggy Stardust & the Spiders (1971–1973)

This is the era that made him immortal. Three albums in two years — *Hunky Dory*, *The Rise and Fall of Ziggy Stardust and the Spiders from Mars*, and *Aladdin Sane* — each one a masterpiece, each one completely different from the last.

*Hunky Dory* (1971) is the hinge point. **Life on Mars?** began as Bowie's sarcastic response to being denied the English lyrics to Claude François's "Comme d'habitude" (which became "My Way" instead). He wrote it as an anti-"My Way" — a surrealist fever dream built on the same chord progression — and it became one of the most beautiful songs of the twentieth century. Rick Wakeman's piano arrangement, performed in a single take, is a miracle of economy.

**Quicksand** is Bowie at his most confessional: wrestling with Crowley, with Buddhism, with his own tendency toward intellectual dilettantism. "I'm not a prophet or a stone-age man, just a mortal with potential of a superman" is as close to a mission statement as Bowie ever wrote. **The Bewlay Brothers** is the album's mysterious closer, a song Bowie himself described as "the first song I've written that I haven't been able to explain." Most interpreters read it as being about Terry.

*Ziggy Stardust* (1972) doesn't need introduction, but the deep cuts do. **Soul Love** is a jazzy, almost playful meditation on different kinds of love — a palate cleanser between the apocalyptic "Five Years" and the extraterrestrial "Moonage Daydream." It's the song most casual listeners skip and most devoted fans cherish.

**Velvet Goldmine** was recorded during the Ziggy sessions and left off the album — a glam-rock stomp about oral sex that was too explicit even for Ziggy. It surfaced as a B-side in 1975 and gave its name to Todd Haynes's 1998 film. The version here is the 2024 remaster of the original session take.

**All the Young Dudes** was written by Bowie for Mott the Hoople, who were about to break up. He offered them "Suffragette City" first; they turned it down. So he went home and wrote them a better song overnight. It saved their career. Bowie's own demo version, included here, is rawer and more personal than the Mott hit.

**Lady Grinning Soul** closes *Aladdin Sane* and might be the most underrated song in the Bowie catalogue. Mike Garson's piano work — half Chopin, half cocktail lounge — is staggering. Bowie's vocal is a masterclass in controlled desire. The song was inspired by a woman named Claudia Lennear, one of Ike Turner's Ikettes, who was also rumoured to be the subject of the Rolling Stones' "Brown Sugar."

**Port of Amsterdam** is Bowie's electrifying cover of Jacques Brel's "Amsterdam," recorded live for a BBC session. It was originally meant to close the Ziggy album before being replaced by "Rock 'n' Roll Suicide." Bowie's delivery — spitting, howling, channelling Brel's theatrical fury — is one of the great live vocal performances of the era.

## Volume IV: The Diamond Duke (1974–1976)

Three personas in three years: the post-apocalyptic Halloween Jack of *Diamond Dogs*, the blue-eyed soul man of *Young Americans*, and the cocaine-emaciated Thin White Duke of *Station to Station*.

**Sweet Thing / Candidate / Sweet Thing (Reprise)** is the centrepiece of *Diamond Dogs* — a nine-minute medley of seedy glamour and urban decay that Bowie originally conceived as part of a stage musical adaptation of *1984* (Orwell's estate refused permission). The saxophone is by Bowie himself, and the guitar by a 17-year-old session player named Alan Parker.

**Who Can I Be Now?** was recorded during the *Young Americans* sessions and then shelved for forty-one years. When it finally appeared on the *Who Can I Be Now?* box set in 2016, critics called it one of the best songs from the sessions — a soaring, string-laden meditation on identity that makes you wonder what other treasures are sitting in the Bowie vault.

**Station to Station** the song is a ten-minute journey from the Thin White Duke's European train station to the cabalistic mysteries of the Tree of Life. Bowie has said he remembers almost nothing of recording it — he was living on red peppers, cocaine, and milk — but the music remembers everything. The transition from the motorik intro to the guitar-driven second half is one of the most thrilling moments in rock.

**Stay** is six minutes of slinky, hypnotic funk-rock driven by Earl Slick's wah-wah guitar. It never became a hit, never got significant radio play, and remains one of the most universally admired tracks among Bowie devotees. Tony Visconti's production is immaculate.

**Wild Is the Wind** was the last track recorded for the album. Bowie's vocal, a reinterpretation of Nina Simone's version (itself a reinterpretation of Johnny Mathis), is often cited as the single greatest vocal performance of his career.

## Volume V: The Berlin Trilogy (1977–1979)

Bowie fled Los Angeles, fled cocaine, fled fame, and arrived in Berlin to make three albums with Brian Eno that changed music permanently. *Low* and *"Heroes"* are routinely cited among the greatest albums ever made. *Lodger* is routinely underrated.

**Sound and Vision** is the trilogy's pop moment — two minutes and fifty-six seconds of shimmering bliss. Bowie wrote the melody in the studio bathroom while Eno and Visconti built the backing track. The first vocal doesn't appear until nearly halfway through the song. It spent three weeks at number three in the UK.

**Warszawa** is the trilogy's soul: a ten-minute ambient piece inspired by the drive from East Berlin to Warsaw, with a vocal sung in a language Bowie invented for the occasion. It sounds like a requiem for a continent. Eno's synthesiser work — particularly the opening four-note motif — has influenced every ambient artist since.

**"Heroes"** was recorded in Hansa Studios, next to the Berlin Wall. Bowie could see a couple meeting every day at the Wall — kissing, despite the guards. The song is about them. Robert Fripp's guitar was recorded through three microphones at different distances; Bowie's vocal was recorded through three microphones at increasing distances, with each mic's gate opening only when he sang louder than the last. The result is one of the most emotional recordings in the history of popular music.

**African Night Flight** from *Lodger* is a manic, percussive track that Bowie recorded in a single take, reading the lyrics from a sheet for the first time. The vocal is deliberately chaotic — stumbling, breathless, ecstatic. Eno's concept for *Lodger* was "world music made by people who have never been there," and this track is the purest expression of that idea.

**Boys Keep Swinging** inverts *"Heroes"*: where that song celebrates romantic defiance against totalitarian surveillance, this one is a sarcastic celebration of patriarchal privilege. The musicians swapped instruments — Dennis Davis played bass, Carlos Alomar played drums — giving the track a deliberately unstable, lurching feel.

## Volume VI: The Pop Star (1980–1984)

**Scary Monsters** (1980) is Bowie's great synthesis: New Wave energy, Berlin sophistication, and pop hooks. Then he made *Let's Dance* (1983) and became the biggest pop star on earth, which he found both lucrative and spiritually deadening.

**Teenage Wildlife** is the hidden treasure of *Scary Monsters* — seven minutes of soaring rock that Bowie allegedly wrote as a rebuke to Gary Numan (who he felt had borrowed too heavily from his Berlin work). Whether or not that's true, the song stands alone as one of his most passionate vocal performances. The guitar solo is credited to Robert Fripp, though there's debate about whether it's actually Chuck Hammer.

**It's No Game (Pt. 1)** opens *Scary Monsters* with Japanese actress Michi Hirota screaming a translation of the lyrics over a wall of distorted guitar. It's one of the most confrontational album openings in rock. The companion piece, **It's No Game (Pt. 2)**, closes the album as a gentle, almost whispered recitation of the same words. Between them, Bowie maps the entire emotional range of the record.

**Under Pressure** emerged from a spontaneous jam session with Queen at Mountain Studios, Montreux. Neither Bowie nor Freddie Mercury particularly enjoyed the collaboration — both were accustomed to total creative control — but the result transcended them both. The bass line, played by John Deacon, is one of the most recognisable in pop music.

**Cat People (Putting Out Fire)** was written for Paul Schrader's horror film and features one of Bowie's most commanding vocal performances: "putting out fire with gasoline." Giorgio Moroder's production wraps the vocal in pulsing synths. Quentin Tarantino would later use the song to devastating effect in *Inglourious Basterds*.

**This Is Not America**, a collaboration with the Pat Metheny Group for the film *The Falcon and the Snowman*, is a masterpiece of restraint. Metheny's guitar and Bowie's vocal create a mood of melancholy patriotism that is genuinely ambiguous — neither celebration nor protest, but something more complex than either.

## Volume VII: The Wilderness Years (1987–1991)

The era nobody wants to talk about, and the era that made everything after it possible. Bowie had to get lost before he could find himself again.

**Loving the Alien** (1984) is the single genuinely great song from the *Tonight* album — seven minutes of synthesiser grandeur about the Crusades and the persistence of religious violence. It's a song that would have fit comfortably on *Scary Monsters* or *Outside*, stranded on an album of Phil Collins-era pop.

**Time Will Crawl** was Bowie's own pick for the best song on *Never Let Me Down* — which is not a high bar, but the song itself is a genuinely affecting meditation on nuclear anxiety. He re-recorded it in 2008 with a string arrangement, and the reworked version is magnificent. The original, included here, has its own rough charm.

Tin Machine was Bowie's experiment in democracy: a band, not a solo project, with guitarist Reeves Gabrels and the Sales brothers. Critics were hostile. Audiences were confused. But the music — particularly **Heaven's in Here**, a seven-minute blues-rock opener, and **Under the God**, a frantic anti-fascist punk track — was genuinely exciting.

**I Can't Read** would later be covered by Bowie himself, acoustically, for the *Ice Storm* soundtrack. The original Tin Machine version is all chaos and frustration. **Prisoner of Love** has a pop accessibility that the rest of the album deliberately avoids. **Crack City** is perhaps the most punk thing Bowie ever recorded.

## Volume VIII: The Art Chameleon (1993–1999)

The nineties were Bowie's second great creative period, though almost nobody noticed at the time. Four albums, each radically different, each building toward something.

**Jump They Say** (1993) was about Terry. Bowie's half-brother had died by suicide in 1985, jumping from the roof of Cane Hill hospital. Bowie channelled the guilt, the helplessness, and the surveillance-state paranoia of institutional care into a jittering, claustrophobic single that reached number nine in the UK.

**1. Outside** (1995) is Bowie's collaboration with Brian Eno on a concept album about art-murder in a dystopian future — a sentence that sounds like parody but describes a genuinely visionary piece of work. **The Hearts Filthy Lesson** is the album's menacing lead single. **Thru' These Architects Eyes** is its soaring pop highlight — perfect vocals, perfect bass, piano solos that arrive like weather. **Strangers When We Meet**, the gorgeous closer, had been recorded earlier for a compilation but was re-recorded by Eno with new depth and warmth.

**Earthling** (1997) was Bowie's drum-and-bass album, and it's considerably better than that description suggests. **I'm Afraid of Americans**, remixed by Trent Reznor with a video directed by Dom and Nic, became the closest thing to a late-period Bowie hit. **Seven Years in Tibet** is hard-hitting electronic rock about madness. **Little Wonder**, inspired by the Seven Dwarfs (the song's lyrics name each one), proved Bowie could do jungle beats without condescension.

**Thursday's Child** (1999) opens *hours...* with a calm, reflective beauty that Bowie's recent work had been too frantic to allow. It's the sound of a man who has finally stopped running.

## Volume IX: The Sage (2002–2004)

Then, inexplicably, Bowie made two of his best albums.

*Heathen* (2002) was recorded in the months after 9/11, and its atmosphere — dread, beauty, a sense of endings — is inseparable from that moment. **Sunday** opens with an unsettling ambient wash before erupting into a track that feels like watching a city burn from a great distance. **5:15 the Angels Have Gone** uses synthetic choirs and mechanical rhythms to create something genuinely uncanny. **Heathen (The Rays)** closes the album with a vocal performance of such power and resignation that it feels like a farewell — though Bowie had, at this point, another fourteen years to live.

*Reality* (2003) is more energetic but equally accomplished. **New Killer Star** is Bowie's most accessible opening track since "Modern Love." **Bring Me the Disco King** is an eight-minute jazz epic that Mike Garson accompanies with piano work of devastating subtlety — the same Garson who played the famously unhinged solo on "Aladdin Sane" thirty years earlier. Here, he plays with heartbreaking restraint.

**Days** is a deceptively simple pop song built on fourteen-bar phrases (not the standard eight or sixteen), giving it a sense of constantly arriving somewhere unexpected. **She'll Drive the Big Car** is the album's hidden gem: a domestic drama of quiet disillusionment, played as new-wave pop.

After the *Reality* tour, Bowie suffered a heart attack on stage in 2004. He retreated from public life entirely.

## Volume X: The Final Act (2013–2016)

Nine years of silence. Then, on his sixty-sixth birthday — 8 January 2013 — Bowie released **Where Are We Now?** without warning. The video showed him in a Berlin artist's studio, eyes closed, surrounded by images of the Hansa neighbourhood where he'd made *"Heroes"* thirty-six years earlier. The internet collectively wept.

*The Next Day* (2013) is Bowie's angriest album. **The Next Day** (the title track) is a furious indictment of religious hypocrisy. **Valentine's Day** is a love song that's actually about a school shooter — the melody is deliberately, chillingly sweet. **I'd Rather Be High** is anti-war pop with a 1960s lilt. **Heat** closes the album with a vocal so quiet and controlled it barely exists, like watching someone leave through a door that's almost closed.

**Sue (Or in a Season of Crime)**, released as a standalone single in 2014, bridges the two final albums. Its jazz-influenced chaos and operatic scope announced that Bowie's farewell would not be quiet.

Then came **★** — the symbol, not the word. The album *Blackstar*, released on 8 January 2016, Bowie's sixty-ninth birthday. He died two days later.

The title track is ten minutes long. It opens with a solar eclipse. The lyrics reference Major Tom, ISIS executions, and the Book of Revelation. The saxophone is by Donny McCaslin's jazz quartet. It sounds like nothing else Bowie ever recorded, and nothing else anyone else ever recorded either.

**Lazarus** — "Look up here, I'm in heaven" — is the most devastating album opener since "Five Years." Bowie knew he was dying when he wrote it. The video shows him in a hospital bed, bandaged, writhing, reaching upward. **'Tis a Pity She Was a Whore** is chaotic jazz-rock named after a Jacobean revenge play. **Girl Loves Me** uses Nadsat (from *A Clockwork Orange*) and Polari (the coded language of London's gay subculture) to create a vocabulary that exists outside ordinary meaning.

**Dollar Days** is the album's most conventionally beautiful song: "If I never see the English evergreens I'm running to, it's nothing to me, it's nothing to see." It is the song of a man saying goodbye to a country he hasn't lived in for forty years, and it is quietly annihilating.

**I Can't Give Everything Away** is the last song on the last album. The harmonica motif that opens it was sampled from *A New Career in a New Town* on *Low* — the Berlin album, the rebirth album, the album that started everything that mattered. The circle closes. The alien goes home.

## The Supplement: EPs, Singles & B-sides

An anthology of album tracks tells one story. The supplement — thirty-two additional tracks drawn from EPs, standalone singles, B-sides, film soundtracks, and collaborative one-offs — tells another, stranger, more intimate story. These are the songs that fell between the albums, the tracks that didn't fit the narrative Bowie was constructing at the time but which, heard now, reveal the full breadth of his restlessness.

**Can't Help Thinking About Me** and **I Dig Everything** (both 1966) are the earliest recordings here — pre-Deram singles that show a teenage Bowie already writing with a specificity and social awareness that his contemporaries lacked. **Karma Man** and **In the Heat of the Morning**, both Deram-era outtakes, hint at the psychedelic direction he might have taken had Space Oddity not intervened.

**The Prettiest Star** (1970) features Marc Bolan — then still Marc Bolan, not yet T. Rex — on lead guitar. The two were friends and rivals; Bolan would die in a car crash in 1977, two weeks after appearing on Bowie's television show. **Conversation Piece**, its companion B-side, is among the most nakedly autobiographical songs Bowie ever wrote: a portrait of loneliness in a bedsit.

The Ziggy and Aladdin Sane eras produced a cluster of non-album singles that are as essential as anything on the albums themselves. **John, I'm Only Dancing** (1972) was considered too sexually ambiguous for American release. **Round and Round**, a Chuck Berry cover, appeared as the B-side of "Drive-In Saturday." **Sorrow**, from the *Pin Ups* covers album, became a number three hit — one of Bowie's biggest UK singles.

**Knock on Wood** (1974) captures the manic energy of the Diamond Dogs tour — Bowie performing Eddie Floyd's soul classic with a live band running on adrenaline and paranoia. **Across the Universe** (1975), the B-side of "Fame," features John Lennon on guitar and backing vocals. The two had recorded it during the *Young Americans* sessions at Electric Lady Studios.

The Bing Crosby duet — **Peace on Earth / Little Drummer Boy** — was recorded in September 1977 for Crosby's Christmas television special. Crosby died five weeks later. The song was released as a single in 1982 and has since become one of the most-played Christmas recordings in history. It is also, quite genuinely, beautiful.

**Alabama Song** (1980) is Bowie's cover of the Brecht/Weill song from *The Rise and Fall of the City of Mahagonny* — a natural extension of the Berlin period's European cabaret influence. **Crystal Japan** (1980), an entirely instrumental single released only in Japan as a sake commercial soundtrack, is a four-minute ambient piece of startling delicacy.

The **Baal EP** (1982) — **Baal's Hymn** and **The Drowned Girl** included here — was Bowie's contribution to a BBC television production of Brecht's first play. It sold modestly and was largely forgotten, but the performances are extraordinary: Bowie channelling Weimar cabaret with absolute commitment. **Dancing in the Street** with Mick Jagger was recorded in a single afternoon for Live Aid (1985). The video is objectively terrible. The single went to number one.

The **Labyrinth** soundtrack (1986) — **Underground**, **Magic Dance**, **As the World Falls Down**, and **Within You** — is Jim Henson's puppet fantasy elevated by Bowie's presence. "Magic Dance" is a novelty song performed with a baby and a room full of goblins; "Within You" is a genuinely anguished ballad about power and loss. **When the Wind Blows** (1986), for the Raymond Briggs nuclear war animation, is similarly underrated.

**Real Cool World** (1992), **Buddha of Suburbia** (1993), and **Pallas Athena** (1993) are scattered soundtrack and non-album contributions from Bowie's early nineties transitional period. **Lucy Can't Dance**, an outtake from the *Black Tie White Noise* sessions, is a swaggering funk-rock track that deserved better than being buried as a bonus cut.

The Heathen-era extras — **Safe**, **Shadow Man**, and **Wood Jackson** — were recorded during the 2001 Toy sessions and the Heathen sessions. "Shadow Man" in particular, a song Bowie first wrote in 1971 and revisited three decades later, is a haunting bridge between the young songwriter and the mature artist.

The **No Plan EP** (2017) — **No Plan**, **Killing a Little Time**, and **When I Met You** — was released posthumously, drawn from the *Lazarus* musical sessions. These are among the very last songs Bowie recorded. "No Plan" is exactly what its title promises: a man facing death without a map, without a scheme, without even the comfort of a persona to hide behind. It is, perhaps, the most honest song he ever wrote.

## A Note on What's Missing

No anthology can contain everything. Notably absent: the *Pin Ups* covers album (1973) beyond the single "Sorrow," the *Buddha of Suburbia* soundtrack in full (1993), and the vast majority of Bowie's collaborative work with Iggy Pop, Lou Reed, and others. Tin Machine's second album is not available on Tidal, which means tracks like "Baby Universal," "Amlapura," and "You Belong in Rock n' Roll" are regrettably absent.

Several versions of tracks are live recordings or remasters rather than original studio takes — this reflects Tidal's catalogue rather than curatorial preference. Where a 2015 or 2017 remaster was the available option, we took it gladly: David Bowie's estate has supervised these remasters with extraordinary care.

The point of an anthology is not completeness. It is *invitation*. If you finish this collection and want more, go listen to *Buddha of Suburbia* in full. Go listen to *The Gouster*, the unreleased predecessor to *Young Americans*. Go listen to Iggy Pop's *The Idiot*, which Bowie produced, and which is essentially a Bowie album in disguise. Go listen to the BBC sessions, the live albums, the box sets. There is always more Bowie. There is, mercifully, always more.
